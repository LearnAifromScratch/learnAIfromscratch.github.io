Just the basics

Usually no trading company will hire new QRs directly unless they have pedigree meaning they come from a famous school. You can however move internally from the 'back office' where you are doing shit work to the 'front office' and perform more shit work (at first) to get into QR if you know the basics.

This is about getting into fintech doing anything like mopping the floors or writing boilerplate code then kicking down the door of QR and doing something everyone said would be impossible for you to do.

What does a QR do?

A good breakdown is here and the same author's PDF floating around from 2011 on algorithmic trading that he was forced to abandon upon signing NDAs to work where he is now. I am also prohibited from writing pretty much anything about it (I'm not a QR I'm just a lackey assistant) but I can say a good QR is an experimental scientist is part Sherlock Holmes, Isaac Newton and a vegas magician who has mastered the art of data analysis such as minimizing all sources of bias and can find a signal in a bunch of noise. They are also responsible for optimizing the entire trading platform and strategy to reduce fees a source of giga bonuses but all of them only care about alpha.

What I can say is it seems most of their time is spent explaining strategy for example they have some specific domain knowledge, use it to find a signal or optimization, and now have to explain and replicate it so others can adapt or sign off on it. The longer the strategy the more planning and meetings.

While they are spending time explaining there's a ton of other work to do that's where you come in as the assistant or junior/trainee.

What does an 'assistant' QR do?

You can easily spot the shit work from the above blog post that a professional scientist making 7 figures would love to pawn off on some eager trainee. Even if there is a small army of teams set up for the QR department there is always something tedious they would rather pay someone else to do for them.

Shit work to them is basically anything that isn't directly involved in finding alpha. Maintenance of the research infrastructure is shit work and especially any kind of software writing and there's always software to write such as monitoring software, trading data capturing and archiving, having to extract some procedure out of a library and rewrite it to be faster, creating and automating all the graphs and visuals for end of day review, wrangling all the new data pipelines to study, preparing all the regulatory compliance and internal reports, there is a continuous stream of side work they would love for you to come around and do for them.

Why would you offer to do this? Because then they have time to show you the magic.

Is this even possible?

If you sell yourself and they want you around they will ask management to sign off and then you are presented with some additional non-disclosure agreements. Even in QR everything is compartmentalized it seems nobody ever has the keys to everything so if it's a big company they'll be willing. I did this for free at first in my own time outside of writing software because it was the only way I could get in.

The need for more researchers is never ending and where I work goes to enormous lengths to try and convince smart people to do this work by throwing massive sums of money at them.

What are the basics?

I assume you can program so all you need is data analysis on noisy but semi-structured data meaning it's JSON, Apache Parquet column-oriented files or a network packet with fields.

  • Probability
    • this is a workbook really not a textbook
    • look who sponsors the course for this book
  • Modern Regression and Data Analysis
    • uses R but Julia/Matlab/Python exists too use whatever you want
  • Intro to Game Theory (optional)
    • helps to understand QR lingo and strategies
    • or try The Mathematics of Poker by Chen/Ankenman

The QRs you choose to learn from will have their own preferred models and sometimes these are physics heat models or they have a compsci/stats degree and are using graphical models or they have a biology background and are using some strange Riemannian geometric model to minimize dimensions. It makes no sense at all to prepare for all these until you find out what your chosen team is actually doing and then you can learn from them directly.

Machine learning is a seperate department and Transformer models are used for things you wouldn't expect like predicting 40 septillion floating point operations so you don't have to use processor resources to calculate them or estimate network packet responses. Anything to speed up data analysis or the trading optimizer is being worked on by highly specialized research teams. It's better to learn ML from them directly and if you have a probability and basic statistical learning background taught in any data analysis/regression book it will be trivial to pick up because you'll be using it at the same time you are learning.

The difference

The difference between corporate software eng and being a 'quant researcher/developer' is night and day in the corporate engineering sector it's all meetings and careful planning in the fly by the seat of your pants world of QR you are needing to rewrite the entire trading strategy from scratch in a single night and it's all pure hacks and wildly fun paying you way more than you'd ever make in the software ghetto.

Game Theoretic Probability

I'm personally interested in Game-Theoretic Probability where essentially all concepts of randomness are thrown out. Probabilities are now forecasts and statistical models are tested with betting and non-stochastic. Everything is much simpler as it's now deterministic and doesn't require measure theory as a foundation. If you've ever been highly skeptical of stock markets being supposedly stochastic instead of a game played by multiple participants then you'll probably want to take this but it's advanced math we have to take graduate analysis to understand it.

Real Analysis

There are a number of amazing analysis texts floating around. The free books here are 'DRIPPED' meaning the Riemann integral has been dropped and very thorough presentations I've never seen elsewhere are given like all the interpretations of a derivative, all the tests for continuity, all the definitions of a limit, etc. These texts were written by Professor Emeritus Andrew Bruckner and his wife Judith who are still active researchers despite being 90 years old and Brian S. Thomson who argues we should modernize the curriculum like dropping Rolle's Theorem. His two books The Calculus Integral and Theory of the Integral are definitely the two best math books I've seen and he tells the reader which 19th century theorems taught in undergrad can be skipped as they are promptly replaced in 21st century graduate instruction.

If you are interested in statistics and probability without the transparent nonsense of randomness such as 'independent and identically distributed' junk then we can begin a curious and possibly foolhardy jump straight into graduate analysis as I will use both Real Analysis by Bruckner and Thomson, Measure, Integration, & Real Analysis by Axler and some functional analysis lectures from MIT to learn 21st century mathematics. If you don't care at all about game-theoretic statistics and want to learn probability as defined by measure theory this is your chance too. If you have only a highschool education, who cares, just try it first. This is not undergraduate it's all redefined completely and abstract. In fact I've never understood the point of prereqs being undergrad and none of it has anything to do with undergrad.
