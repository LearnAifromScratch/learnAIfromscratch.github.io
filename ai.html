Intro

In 2023 the education gods have blessed us with complete access to Reinforcement Learning which is the futuristic AI you likely imagined meaning autonomous cars/robots/space ships that can explore their environment and make decisions by themselves. We don't require access to a GPU or cloud hardware as a lot of RL is essentially dynamic programming something we already know how to do but will be taught in the course anyway. The entire course and all prereqs are done in the Julia programming language.

Plan

The goal is self-directed research in RL making whatever it is you want to build so take what you want from this curriculum you don't have to do it all.

  • CMU's 16-745 Optimal Control and RL

  • Some DL material from 10-703 Deep RL

  • Linear Algebra in Julia here and Matrix Calculus

    • MIT's statistical theory of RL book

Option: Deep Learning

If you just want to learn Deep Learning CMU's 11-785 Intro to DL is fully open with YouTube lectures and assignments available, many of which are practicing on Kaggle. MIT's course is also fully open in fact I think every university has their DL courses open right now because there is no chance of you being able to do it on a home system unless you use tinygrad and buy the hilarious pre-order tinybox for $100 which is worth $15k (I'm not endorsing any kind of pre-order just pointing out it exists). There is also depth first learning Neural ODEs and whenever I can I try and maintain that site because it's so good.

I didn't include DL here except for some of the Deep RL because it req access to a ton of resources which I imagine people don't have when they take this. DL theory of course you can create all kinds of new neural networks on your own like the current researchers doing 'liquid neural networks' if that kind of thing is your bag. CMU if you search their public Panopto has the entire (non)convex optimization courses available right now, that information I could not find anywhere else and is essentially 25 lectures how to select the pytorch optimize feature. Convex optimization once upon a time was heavily used until neural networks took over everything which are not convex.

Option: Machine Learning

There is an applied course by the guy who maintains scikit-learn:

  • COMS-W4995 Applied Machine Learning Columbia
    • 21 lectures on YouTube
    • Homework on GitHub
    • Books recommended:
      • Applied Predictive Modeling [APM]
      • Intro to Machine Learning w/Python [IMLP]
      • Fundamentals of Data Visualization

My choice to learn the theory is the first 20 chapters of the (free) book Understanding Machine Learning: From Theory to Algorithms w/lectures. It's still taught by the author at Waterloo in 2021 and CMU's PhD track intro course 10-715. There's a solutions manual for the few exercises in the book which will be an exercise in itself to try and understand. He doesn't assume a background and goes through all the math notation.

Those machine learning theory lectures are kind of amazing how he can explain it all and the theory isn't going to change so if you're interested try it but I won't go through it here.
