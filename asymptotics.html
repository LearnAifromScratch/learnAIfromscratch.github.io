Exponential & Logarithmic Functions

Explained here (Clemson Math 1060 sec 1.3). These Clemson U lectures average ~20m and are just the right difficulty if you forgot high school like most people so gaps will be filled in and there's lot's of constructions instead of descriptive/abstract definitions.

Logarithmic inverses, why does \(\large b^{log_b{(x)}} = x\)?

  • \(\large 2^{log_2{(1)}} = 2^0 = 1\)
  • \(\large 2^{log_2{(2})} = 2^1 = 2\)
  • \(\large 2^{log_2{(3})} \approx 2^{1.6} \approx 3\)
  • \(\large 2^{log_2{(4})} = 2^2 = 4\)

A base e log or ln(x) is often used in computer science literature instead of base 2 because \(\large log_{2}(x) = \frac{ln(x)}{ln(2)}\) meaning you are off only by a constant factor and now can enjoy all the nice properties ex and ln(x) have for doing quick approximations by hand. For small inputs where x2 is evaluated smaller than x then ex can be approximated with 1 + x and ln(1 - x) can be approximated with -x. To see how etime = growth and ln(growth) = time then read this.

Notice how a log is a linearization of an exponent. One example is if you log 2x then log(2x) = xlog(2) and you mapped a quadratic to a linear space.

Trig Functions

Examples are frequently done using trig functions in all literature for analysis/calculus.

If you don't remember trig at all try this (Math 1060 sec 1.4) review of Trig functions and their inverses. Skip through and remind yourself that sin-1 is not 1/sin. Where do those square root x/y coords come from on the unit circle? Explained in the Trig Boot Camp (Brown University) which also has a YouTube lecture. Two right angle triangles (deg 30/60/90 and 45/45/90) need to be scaled down so the hypotenuse is 1 to fit on the unit circle.

We have just seen another linearization. Watch a few minutes of this (Wildberger Rational Trig) starting @5:56 to see motion around a nonlinear curve being mapped to the linear motion of the two axis moving back and forth.

Derivatives

We mostly need integration, once you have an idea what derivatives are skip directly to Integrals unless you need this for AI.

  • Derivative (Math 1060 3.1) shows the geometry of a derivative at a point.
  • Derivative as a Function (Math 1060 3.2.1) To see how the dy/dx Leibniz notation works watch this (MIT 18.06) starting at 3:27 for only a few minutes. The key relationship is dy = 6dx or dy/dx = 6 meaning you linearize x2 to 2x.
  • Derivative Rules (Math 1060 3.3) the usual formulas, higher-order derivatives, ex is it's own derivative, and remark that differentiation is a linear operator.

These are all optional but presented well, the whole course is worth doing if you wanted.

  • Product Rule (Math 1060 3.4) (fg)' = f'g + fg' and (f/g)' = (f'g - fg')/g2
  • Trig Derivatives (Math 1060 3.6) covers reciprocal limits too. Lightly skim this just so you know the derivative of sinx is cosx and that if you take the derivative of sinx 4x then it's a cycle that returns to sinx.
  • Rates of Change (Math 1060 3.6) explains real life calculus meaning economic cost function derivatives and physics how the derivative of f(time) = position gives you f(time) = velocity. You can undo the velocity derivative to go back to the position function using indefinite integration.
  • Chain Rule (Math 1060 3.7) the derivative of function composition or f'(g)*g'
  • Derivatives of Log/Exp (Math 1060 3.8)
