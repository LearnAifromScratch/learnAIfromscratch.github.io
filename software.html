Fintech recruiting in 2024

I often meet up with software recruiters in finance who tell me a tale of woe how much harder it's become to find candidates. I asked them what exactly do you want these days for basic new grad style candidates and would you look at resumes without a degree. The following curriculum is a result of these conversations which are ongoing as I've been toying with the idea can I teach someone to do what we do here from scratch so they don't have to spend $90k or w/e the total cost is now for undergrad at a university. I have no intention of creating a new filter or mandatory hoop people must jump through this is simply a collection of free materials to optionally take if you don't have a degree.

The recruiters told me if they could find a non-degree candidate that knows the entire CPU-arch execution pipeline and can answer any question about how an eviction from the cache might happen (like a hash collision) then they get a point. If they know a language and can explain how it works precisely including it's managed runtime if it has one then that's another point. If they have provable problem solving skills typically taught by competitive programming where given a seemingly impossible problem the candidate can at least work to a non-optimized solution without immediately giving up then that's another point. Finally if they can do all of the above with a very high standard of code quality then that is the perfect introductory candidate who can be taught anything else needed.

There's many reasons why you'd want to work for trading firms. Valkyrie in Chicago will hire juniors for example but the rest will as well if you know the material here. My reasons are paid education. You get paid to go from technician to the Formula 1 race car engineer of software development.

The work

Everything is bespoke from the server metal up where you are expected to know or quickly learn everything about L1/L2 cache and Intel features like Intel RDT (CAT,CMT) or Processor Trace. Not all the work is pure optimization these big funds now have large suites of custom client software and even roll their own internal email, code review software, even the compiler is completely custom where I work. You typically start out working on customer trading software or at the beginning of the critical path where hundreds of paid data feeds are coming in as a massive end of trading day dump or coming in continuously that have to be parsed in the most efficient possible strategy. One example is those 'no fee trading' middle-man brokerages like Robinhood they are likely selling their entire order book to a major HFT firm meaning someone had to write the infrastructure for that order book to be parsed, be fed into a trading optimizer to find the lowest fee buy strategy then sell it back to Robinhood within microseconds. You are trained how to do this of course which is why selection is so competitive they need developers who can learn.

Recruiters

Jove Intl, Oxford Knight, Reload Search, and ex-employees who have quit to become highly paid one-man recruiting shops most of whom are the one's I've been talking to after running into them at bars after work and at events. They hire internationally for Chicago, NYC, Austin, London, Sydney, Hong Kong, Mumbai, Shanghai, Tokyo and now Singapore seems to be the location everyone is opening offices these days.

Selection

They told me they go over all resumes, there's no automated ATS or applicant tracking system. USACO training stands out, so-called modern C++ meaning C++17/20/23 stands out but is not necessary as they will train you. You never sell yourself as a junior, companies will hire anyone who knows how to sell themselves and can pass the technical competence filters. You always pretend to be employed writing software even if this means setting up a personal page declaring yourself a freelancer on Upwork actively looking for contract work. Candidates are sometimes ranked, so you have to be better than everyone else who would automatically rank higher (degrees, work experience) which is possible through sheer determination because the hiring pool for entry level candidates who can both pass the interviews and are willing to stay on and become senior is very limited right now. Referals do exist but have consequences meaning you are personally vouching for someone so nobody is going to hand out a referral to a stranger online.

Campus recruiting

Getting more and more monopolized by big tech and big gov contractors that manipulate school guidelines by pressuring students with 2-week expiring offers (aka exploding offers) so it is a struggle to find any of the good grads to do this kind of work. Professors sometimes act like recruiters now too receiving a form of commission setting up their students with companies further draining the hiring and internship pool. The available roles seem like they pay less than big tech when you forget you can invest your bonus in the high-yield investment fund too so that's another reason many new grads don't apply.

Phone screens

I don't know if they still do this but one Chicago company was notorious for firing off 3 problems to solve on Hackerrank seconds after you submitted an application. The phone screen is usually a recruiter asking are you legal to work and if not what credentials do you have because in some countries like the US you will need a reason for them to issue you a work visa for a job that can't be given to a local so a degree or equivalent work experience. They will of course dodge salary questions with the tried and true 'well I hire for x amount of countries so don't have that exact information right now' but in my experience they offer a range at least.

If you have been vouched for, which is very common in the finance world, then you skip the second phone interview and come in for a casual meeting usually about whatever you previously built and they may ask you about details of the language you claim to be an expert in. Any jargon you use they will ask you to define on the spot to catch you bluffing. If for whatever reasons you're asked to write software it will be to implement atoi which is the new fizzbuzz filter everyone has adopted and tests how careless of a developer you are meaning you don't ask any questions about requirements and start writing code immediately. We were told if we were to personally vouch and refer candidates for an interview then our friends we vouched for could expect the above. There's of a course a catch, if we refer someone and they fail miserably meaning we are careless with referals, then we have to cover the cost of the interview which is thousands of dollars so you won't find anyone handing out referals to people they don't know.

If not vouched for expect the second zoom/phone call to be a typical algorithm style interview. Code correctness and caution is heavily favored over adding complexity to squeeze out some meaningless optimization of constant factors. As a personal anecdote these interview tasks are often designed as a trap to once again see how careless of a developer you are the problems I've seen have an obvious area of optimization to take that will open a performance sink elsewhere if you aren't taking your time thinking through the whole original problem space. Prime example they will try and get you with is cache invalidation. If they're still interested you'll be paid to come out for on-site 'culture fit' job shadowing and sometimes multiple more rounds of interviews but it depends on the country in Hong Kong I had a brief on-site meeting immediately followed by an employment contract offer. Hiring someone is an investment it will typically take 3 months for them to be productive and if you're being paid $200k/yr that's $50k they have spent hoping you will turn out to be a good hire so now you know why there is all these hoops to jump through.

Find a mentor

Once you are in and develop a routine walk over to the department where all the money is being made and find a mentor to teach you by asking them to give you the work they don't like doing because you want to learn it all from scratch. All you have to do is be the reliable person they turn to whenever some (tedious at first) work needs to be done and if this work over time becomes increasingly more skilled then you have found the right mentor and you are set for life because they will eventually want you to work with them once you have proven to be indispensible and insist you be transferred internally where you will double your salary. If they get promoted you get promoted, if they go off and start a new company you are the first hire. This can be tricky because of signing multiple NDAs even between internal departments but if you are serious about wanting to learn they will tell management to make it happen. I actually took all the above advice from a comedian many years ago and my life would not be the same if I hadn't watched that throwaway clip at 3am because I always assumed there was no way I could ever do this kind of work.

If you're not interested in finance there are a few companies that mentor developers sometimes even direct from local high schools and build them to senior developers. One is in New Hampshire called Northwoods Software. If you live in Egypt, Georgia, North Macedonia, Albania, Latvia, or Bosnia you can work remotely for Scandiweb (Latvia). The pay is only 500-600 Euros per month but you don't know anything yet so are being paid for direct mentorship to become a senior developer. LinkedIn is trash I've only ever used it to harvest recruiter offers. Of course not all fintech outfits are worth working at, do your own research on what kind of NDAs they may make you sign though the video game industry does the same anti-competitor working clause in all their employment contracts and everyone ignores them unless you make your own competing company then expect to be sued.

Explains that almost all industry popular languages are virtually identical and demystifies all the OOP jargon. We use a web-based stacker to see exactly how programs execute.

MIT course and book on how to design software people want to use.

  • Queueing theory

If you write enough software eventually you discover that almost everything is some kind of queue pattern. This is another workbook that's Q&A style not a giant theory text.

  • Advanced DBMS (OLAP)

Here we learn kernel bypass methods for low latency networking, multicore programming, hashing/join algorithms, search space complexity reduction, and modern distributed application architecture like microservices. We can also look at their AI tuning seminar for relational dbms optimization knob tuning. Most of the assignments are finishing features in C++ for a teaching database with a Rust written optimizer but there's nothing stopping you from using whatever language you prefer.

We will learn everything we can about C++ profiling and performance engineering taking some material on cache architecture from ETH Zurich and some compiler lectures about garbage collected runtimes for other languages.

Gitless

We'll come back to this course after we learn git. There's a GUI for git you don't have to use the command line interface.

Many companies today use a customized Mercurial fork or rewritten clone instead of git like Meta's rust written EdenFS and Sapling SCM because git was originally developed to take advantage of linux file system optimizations ergo is conceptually attached to a file system while Mercurial can be easily adapted to be stuffed in a database, distributed or run on any OS. In a Mercurial style of version control, you probably don't care about individual commits so you don't even add comments to commits you instead care about entire branch development.

Let's learn what git actually is by watching MIT's version control lecture from it's course The Missing Semester of Your CS Education. Lecture starts ~7m in. So far the data structure and model is trivial to understand if you did any intro course here such as CS19 learnings about DAGs.

Proceed to gitless.com and navigate to research or this page on MIT's software design group. You will find this talk about the problems with git design. The guy giving this talk was the PhD student of the prof we watched in the conceptual design lectures and now is a co-founder of Aleph some fintech data startup (which is hiring, maybe you can impress him with your conceptual design skills).

Big-O explained

The USACO guide has a good introduction to big-O and a resource we can use where running time is actually explained is the beginning chapters of Functional Data Structures by Prof Ragde at Waterloo try reading the first few chapters up to 2.2.

The formal definition is:

\(f(x) \le c\cdot g(x)\) for all inputs \(x \ge x_0\) as x (inputs) goes to infinity. If that inequality holds then f(x) is in O(g(x)) which represents a worse-case scenario upper bound. A 'best-case' (wishful thinking) scenario for insertion sort is you don't have to do anything but attach the element to the already sorted list and the worst-case is the entire list is in reverse order and every element looked at and moved.

f() function is a cost function of time or resources derived from a cost model. It's where you count up every constant operation and loop costs by analyzing source. This is not always easy since what costs do you assign when the code triggers garbage collection or triggers memory allocation because an object has been created deep in some library code.

The g() function is chosen from a list of math functions that represent a 'family of functions'. A family means all functions that are linear, all functions that are quadratic, all functions that are linearithmic etc. It actually is a specific list as we'll soon see.

Why are constants hidden in big-O? If you read SICP their example is 16 and 8 both differ by a factor of 2 and 8 but we don't care which factor. In real life the constant factors do matter if they are hiding a lot of operations. A classic example of this is when Knuth was writing the Stanford GraphBase (contents are in Vol 4A of TAOCP) and his real-world testing of popular graph algorithms found that 'Fibonacci heaps' that were analyzed using a technique called amortized analysis weren't any better than Kruskal's old algorithm because the analysis was hiding large constant operations. One way to reduce constants is to do consecutive rounds of a linear algorithm such as cleaning up a graph removing cycles because O(n + n) or O(2n) is still O(n).

Big-O doesn't represent growth since if f(x) = x2 it does not grow to be quadratic, f(x) already is quadratic, thus f() is in \(O(n^{2})\) or upper bounded by every quadratic function. Typically in algorithm analysis the inputs are supposed to be 'near infinite' which is why n is used instead of x in f(n) to signal the difference in inputs but here in the USACO guide we can see that if the input is small then we can get away with some very bad runtimes even exponential.

Problem complexity

Before we start trying to solve problems watch this Erik Demaine lecture and skip to 09:00. We want to know what polynomial time is, what NP means, what satisfiability means and this is a good enough ~30m crash course in complexity as these concepts come up all the time in algorithm literature. He tells us most decision problems can't be solved by an algorithm which ruins the AI superintelligence dystopia. When he translates Super Mario Bros to a 3SAT decision problem we can see that almost every algorithm you can think of can be transformed to a decision problem. You don't have to keep watching this but there's an interesting intro to constraint logic/graphs. Now you can explain to someone P=NP means all the nondeterministic polynomial time algorithms that always 'lucky guess' correctly or have a magic oracle can be solved by an algorithm in deterministic polynomial time which seems impossible.

The rest of this course I won't do here but the book he wrote that goes with the lectures looks excellent covering topics in economics too.
