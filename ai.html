Intro

The current state of AI is transformer architecture which have all been trained by self-supervised learning where they are now even creating their own optimized architecture by themselves and showing emergent properties meaning they posess abilities they weren't designed for. The whole point of AI was the model will one day build itself a better model and that day is today.

There's a new field called Mechanistic Interpretability which is essentially reverse engineering AI. Neel Nanda has helpfully produced a concrete curriculum for this and even offers mentorship in this field through the ML Alignment & Theory Scholars program so we may as well start there.

Limits of AI

Current popular AI is all foundation models like GPT-n, DALL-E etc. Have you wondered if we had infinite resources, infinite data, perfect training algorithms with no errors, can we use this type of model for everything? Someone with help from the Beijing Academy of AI used category theory to model this scenario to see what is possible.

PreReqs

Looking at his guide for prereqs he provides the material for transformer architecture but first we need to know the following:

  • Linear algebra
  • Probability basics
  • Multivariable calculus basics

All these topics are covered by MIT's 18.065 Matrix Methods and MIT's Deep Learning boot camp to fill in the geometry so we can visualize what we're doing. However that course assumes you've already had a multivariable calculus background, which means you had a scalar calculus background, and some kind of undergrad linear algebra introduction.

We can get both by auditing Evan Chen's Napkin which prefers to use the tools of graduate mathematics because undergrad math is a convoluted mess that nobody actually uses. For example the Riemann integral was obsoleted well over 100+ years ago but they hilariously make you take it in every undergrad course from Calc I to Analysis II even teaching you nonsense like 'improper integrals' which don't exist. The Lebesgue integral replaced it, then in the (1960s?) the Henstock-Kurzweil integral replaced the Lebesgue integral by generalizing it as a special case and the proofs for these things are simple once all the machinery of a measure space is set up. They're nothing like undergrad proofs. Probability is another mess that is absolutely trivial to understand once you have a measurable space and is a bunch of garbage when you don't.

Mechanistic Interpretability is all about spotting patterns at a high level while we peel off transformations (matrices) so we should begin with math that generalizes everything to it's behavior.
