Intro

This is a modern math workshop meaning I pruned all the legacy junk still taught in undergrad:

  • The Riemann integral you discover on your first day of grad instruction is a totally inadequate theory and has been replaced over a century ago and no mathematician since has used it. They then tell you there is no such thing as 'proper' or 'improper' integrals and you don't need to 'set up the integral' like you had to do in undergrad Physics and other courses. Sorry no refunds.
  • Calculus courses/books contain filler like the method of partial fractions which is a tedious algebraic exercise and not actually part of the theory. Most of the exercises also have nothing to do with understanding the theory and are just trig function drills or cherry picked problems you will never encounter.
  • You will never use echelon forms, free and pivot varibles, explicitly compute inverse matrices, and probably never work with matrices that are tables of numbers. Matrices become much more abstract concepts such as linear operators on functions so Ax = b (matrix * vector = vector) becomes (linear operator)*(lambda function) = f(vector input).

I included programming whenever possible such as learning the Lean proof assistant so you get instant feedback if your proof is at least logically consistent. We can't do all the proofs in Lean (maybe you could with Bishop's Constructive Analysis) but learning proofs is done through imitation, you see what others have done and mimic them first then you can write your own.

Prove the laws of multiplication

We'll do this in the Lean proof assistant too.

Prove n * 1 = n

The definition of multiplication: mn is n + n repeated m times so n1 is 1 + 1 repeated n times so 11 + 12 + … + 1n = n

We will need this result for the other proofs.

Prove the distributive law: (k + n)m = km + nm

km is m + m repeated k times or m1 + … + mk and (k + n)m means m + m repeated (k + n) times. Distributing m in (k + n)m we get: ((m1 + m +…mk) + (m1 + m +…mn)) and now the left side matches the right side since km + nm is the same when expanded.

Prove the associative law: (kn)m = k(nm)

(kn)m is m + m repeated kn times and kn is n + n repeated k times:

(n1 + … + nk)m or (mn1 + … + mnk) using distributive law.

Right hand side: k(nm) is nm + nm repeated k times (nm1 + … + nmk) and factor out m, both sides of the equation are the same.

Prove the commutative law: nm = mn

First let's prove n1 = 1n or 1n = n. We proved n1 = n already, so we can use substitution to replace n in 1n.

1n is 1(11 + .. + 1n) and using the distributive law this is (11 + .. + 1n) or n. So now we have n1 = 1n.

Looking at the right side: mn is (n1 + … + nm)

Factor out n: n(11 + .. + 1m) and a sum of 1's up to m is m, so we have nm = nm.

Descartes on why you should learn math

Leibniz's 'calculus of thought' or precise rules of mental reasoning can also be found in a different form in the preface of Descartes Discours de la Methode. Every modern copy extracts the preface of the book, treating it as the actual book removing his geometry, optics and metereology work which we will shortly see are mandatory for understanding Descartes method. The preface is 6 parts hastily slapped together by Descartes because he was pressed for time where only Part Six, One and Two are the most interesting the rest seem totally random like a long discussion on blood circulation and metaphysics. The best translation is by Ian Maclean for Oxford's World Classics and can be found on library genesis where every translation from 1600s French colloqialisms has an accompanying commentary at the back of the book. The famous quote in this book 'I think, therefore I am' was written as a refutation to Aristotle and his syllogisms as an example that is logically faulty but somehow this quote circulates without pointing out it's context.

However not included in that book is the chapters on geometry, and all the correspondence Descartes wrote after he was 'attacked in pamphlets being circulated'. Descartes hated reading long books, and hated school. This is why he routinely writes don't worry reader, the following isn't too long in many of his chapters hoping to convince the reader to actually read it. His opinion was the best method of mental reasoning was in the geometry texts of Archimedes, Pappus, Apollonius 'and others', because they produced self-evident arguments 'not quite easily known' that don't exactly follow a straight line of deduction. He explains Archimedes comparison of the sphere to a polyhedron in order to learn more about the sphere 'directs the light of nature in such a way that we pay particular attention' and that even if the reader denied the results, they could be pointed to similar results from previous proofs to show it must be true. In other words what he means by this is discoveries made this way aren't always direct consequences of previous deductions where the geometer follows A to B, but independent discoveries done with analysis that once found can always be proven in the framework of geometry by pointing back to some previous proofs as a source of truth. He preferred this kind of demonstratable proof that couldn't be refuted compared to philosophy which considered every possible sides of an argument and produced no truth.

He then laments that almost nobody reads those texts, because they are lengthy and require special attentiveness so are only known by a handful of people. He also laments that discoveries aren't made in formal geometry they're made in analysis (Pappus' projective geometry) so the reader doesn't get to see how the discovery was made only the final results, which do not proceed from A to B, and this is why he wrote his appendix on cartesian geometry the whole point was to create a new analysis that could be easily learned and demonstrate this way of thinking of 'discovering the truth'. He was especially thrilled how short it was. The others on optics and metereology were again simply used as examples how you would go about using his method to make discoveries, and connect directly to the preface about his method yet these are all omitted today.

This is why Isaac Newton attacked Descartes and spent the rest of life trying to refute his philosophy on only learning analysis, because he claimed the analysis wasn't important at all, and that anybody could have figured it out independently just screwing around in their spare time like he did, and that's why the ancients had largely hidden it preferring to show to final proofs in other words no results in math could exist without synthesis meaning demonstrate a proof. Maybe he's right considering the analysis he used of infinitesimals during that time was unproven hacks he did for self-entertainment while on plague lockdown but when it came time to write the Principia he had to prove the analysis, which nobody else could, so performed synthesis showing a limit can exist in the framework of geometry.

Explanation

Without reading further I know of no better explanation of what's going on than the papers of Per Martin-Lof, reading specifically the paper On the Meanings of the Logical Constants and the Justifications of the Logical Laws. It's 25 pages, a set of three lectures he gave that were transcribed. Skim through them leisurely.

1st lecture

Reading the lectures (25 page pdf). On page 3 that turnstile notation is precisely defined on page 8 in the second lecture.

Second time reading this paper I forgot how good it really is, on page 4 he is defining proposition as semantics, meaning a proposition is a complete expression, and from programming we know an expression is something we can 'passively recognize as the same in it's manifold (many) occurrences and actively reproduce in many copies'. Propositions are further defined later in the lectures on page 9.

Page 5, he begins to define judgements as the act of knowing, if you have ever watched Robert Harper lectures on type theory he uses this terminology of assertions being true meaning you have a proof of it or in other words a piece of knowlege, an object which is the proof of A and B. If a proof is an object, then you have a notion of an algebraic structure such as equating two proofs as equal, for example conjunction in algebra is A \(\le\) B or 'a & b', so if you have an algebraic structure you have a model of computation and if you have a model of computation then you have a program and proofs are programs. Page 5 also defines what logic is: the theory of demonstrative knowledge, studying these pieces of knowledge and how they are interrelated. The ancient Greek definition being comprehend (seize or grasp) and know (see). You either know (affirm) or you don't (deny). A is true or is false. You have a piece of knowledge which is an object that constructs how A is true. You have a proof that A is true.

2nd lecture

Aristotle's term white is defined here and means a trope (properties).

Page 9 the turnstile binary relation notation \(\vdash\) from Frege: the vertical judgement (the knowledge force on the object, or the way the subject relates to the object) and the horizontal (content) affirmation. Together turnstile means: 'I know |' and 'is true -' and turnstile is usually called the assertion sign. P \(\vdash\) Q is read: From P, I know that Q is true, or Q is derivable from P, or Q is provable from P.

Page 8 and 9 there are deconstructed examples of why the judgement stroke in Frege's notation is unnecessary, if you return to page 7 all it means is if you say 'A is true' there is no need to also say 'I know A is true' because it is implicit. A is true is a judgement and a judgement means you have the knowledge to judge something true.

Page 9 and 10 we get into the meaning of what a proof really is. Initially he shows proof is an act not an object, but eventually equates proof with knowledge, and a judgement is a piece of knowledge so is an object. Page 11 we start to see the various judgement forms that will come up in the CMU course on logic and math except fully explained. Propositions are brought up again and verifying they are true.

3rd lecture

We learn why you can assume things are true/proven, because of the same reasons why 'I know' in 'I know A is true' is unneccessary because if A is true, by definition of evident judgements, you already know it's true like in Frege's assertion notation it is there even if not made explicit.

On page 17 the upside down T or up tack means always false or impossible. A logical consequence can have all true antecedents with the consequence still false and you can probably think up a dozen examples in your head of trying to deduce things that are true but the consequence is false. There is no possible proof where A being true infers it is always false, unless A is of course false. The more you read of this paper the more everything starts to make sense.

Further on page 17 we get the rules of inference starting with implication, and, or, negation. Read these as you read chapter 2 in the Logic & Proof book and they will make sense. This style of chains of reasoning where the premise(s) are descending is called natural deduction.

If you've never seen sets watch this but if you did CS19 you know what they are.

A set we already know it's an unordered collection, a data structure. The notation x \(\in\) G means an arbitrary element x inside the set G. The open set definition means no matter what element you choose in that set you can find an interval that contains it. The epsilon/delta notation, epsilon represents a tiny displacement on the y-axis, delta represents a tiny displacement on the x-axis or regular number line. The interval created from a tiny displacement around x will always be in G is what that notation is saying. The subset notation (thing) \(\subset G\) means thing is contained within G. We'll learn this again and again throughout the book.

The definition of a closed set if it's complement is open, imagine the interval [3,4] it's compliment is the rest of the real line without the [3,4] interval. You now have a real line with a bound, and any interval with a bound is open as you can't actually reach 3 to 4 the end points of (-infinity, 3) and (4, infinity+) but it is possible to sandwich in approximations to 3 and 4 using tiny displacements that approach them, the definition of an open set.
