Why this exists

I often meet up with software recruiters in finance who tell me a tale of woe how much harder it's become to find candidates. I asked them what exactly do you want these days for basic new grad style candidates and would you look at resumes without a degree. The following curriculum is a result of these conversations which are ongoing as I've been toying with the idea can I teach someone to do what we do here from scratch so they don't have to spend $90k or w/e the total cost is now for undergrad at a university. I have no intention of creating a new filter or mandatory hoop people must jump through this is simply a collection of free materials to optionally take if you don't have a degree.

The recruiters told me if they could find a non-degree candidate that knows the entire CPU-arch execution pipeline and can answer any question about how an eviction from the cache might happen (like a hash collision) then they get a point. If they know a language and can explain how it works precisely including it's managed runtime if it has one then that's another point. If they have provable problem solving skills typically taught by competitive programming where given a seemingly impossible problem the candidate can at least work to a non-optimized solution without immediately giving up then that's another point. Finally if they can do all of the above with a very high standard of code quality then that is the perfect introductory candidate.

There's many reasons why you'd want to work for trading firms. Valkyrie in Chicago will hire juniors explicitly for example but the rest will as well if you know the material here. My reasons are paid education. You get paid to go from technician to the Formula 1 race car engineer of software development. These outfits also thrive in volatility there's never any lay offs that I have seen (yet).

The work

Everything is bespoke from the server metal up where you are expected to know or quickly learn everything about L1/L2 cache and Intel features like Intel RDT (CAT,CMT) or Processor Trace. Not all the work is pure optimization these big funds now have large suites of custom client software and even roll their own internal email, code review software, everything is custom. There is constantly new software to write and rewrite from scratch when new hardware features are released. You typically start out where I started which was the proprietary data pipeline or top of the critical path where hundreds of paid data feeds are coming in as a massive end of trading day dump or coming in continuously that have to be cleaned of noise and parsed in the most efficient possible strategy because even increasing by a tiny optimization means the difference in millions of dollars. For example those 'no fee trading' consumer grade middle-man brokerages like Robinhood they are likely selling their entire order book to a major HFT firm that executes all the buy orders meaning book comes in with buy orders, trading optimizer must figure out how to buy the security for the lowest fees, then sell this security to the consumer within small fractions of a second. Of course whatever is being bought or sold on that consumer book is yet another data feed to add to whatever strategy the HFT is doing and they can predict what the book will be in an hour and have all those assets ready for sale. From the data dungeon you make your way to the tip of the critical path with experience.

Recruiters

Jove Intl, Oxford Knight, Reload Search, and ex-employees who have quit to become highly paid one-man recruiting shops most of whom are the one's I've been talking to after running into them at bars after work and at events. They hire internationally for Chicago, NYC, London, Sydney, Hong Kong, Mumbai, Shanghai, Tokyo and now Singapore seems to be the location everyone is opening offices these days.

Selection

They told me they go over all resumes, there's no automated system. USACO training stands out, so-called modern C++ meaning C++17/20/23 stands out. You never sell yourself as a junior, companies will hire anyone who knows how to sell themselves and can pass the technical competence filters. You always pretend to be employed writing software even if this means setting up a personal page declaring yourself a freelancer on Upwork actively looking for contract work. Candidates are sometimes ranked, so you have to be better than everyone else who would automatically rank higher (degrees, work experience) which is possible through sheer determination because the hiring pool for entry level candidates who can both pass the interviews and are willing to stay on and become senior is very limited right now. Nobody I know of has a referral scheme in fact it's a risk handing out a referral so if you see some stranger offering a referral it's a scam.

Campus recruiting

Getting more and more monopolized by big tech and big gov contractors that manipulate school guidelines by pressuring students with 2-week expiring offers (aka exploding offers) so it is a struggle to find any of the good grads to do this kind of work. Professors sometimes act like recruiters now too receiving a form of commission setting up their students with companies further draining the hiring and internship pool. The available quant dev type roles are limited and seem like they pay less than big tech when you forget you can invest your bonus in the high-yield investment fund too so that's another reason many new grads skip the world of finance.

Phone screens

I don't know if they still do this but one Chicago company was notorious for firing off 3 problems to solve on Hackerrank seconds after you submitted an application. The phone screen is usually a recruiter asking are you legal to work and if not what credentials do you have because in some countries like the US you will need a reason for them to issue you a work visa for a job that can't be given to a local so a degree or equivalent work experience. They will of course dodge salary questions with the tried and true 'well I hire for x amount of countries so don't have that exact information right now' but in my experience they offer a range at least.

Now phone screen #2.

If you have been vouched for, which is very common in the finance world, then your phone interview will be something simple such as implement atoi for the sole purpose of seeing if you even bother asking questions to clarify the requirements or work through edge cases. This is to guage how careless you are as a developer because mistakes cost money and atoi is essentially the new fizz buzz filter everyone has adopted. After expect to be asked about your chosen language's runtime in detail and any jargon you use they will ask you to define it on the spot to catch you bluffing. This is harder than it sounds because it's like an oral general exam in grad school where a committee tests your knowledge on the spot but nobody with the kind of experience who would be given this interview style should fail this. That's it you're in. Yes, it's actually that easy once you come vouched for because it's a small industry and everyone knows each other.

However if you are an unknown therefore a risk to hire then expect a longer interview with a collaborative design. Code correctness and caution is heavily favored over adding complexity to squeeze out some meaningless optimization of constant factors. As a personal anecdote these interview tasks are often designed as a trap to once again see how careless of a developer you are the problems I've seen have an obvious area of optimization to take that will open up a bug somewhere else if you aren't taking your time thinking through the whole problem space. In the end they really just want to see what you are like to work with and that you have high standards. If you're unknown but they're still interested you'll be paid to come out for on-site 'culture fit' job shadowing too and multiple more rounds of interviews.

Find a mentor

Once you are in and survive long enough to develop a routine walk over to the department where all the money is being made and find a mentor to teach you by asking them to give you the work they don't like doing because you want to learn it all from scratch. All you have to do is be the reliable person they turn to whenever some (tedious at first) work needs to be done and if this work over time becomes increasingly more skilled then you have found the right mentor and you are set for life because they will eventually want you to work with them once you have proven to be indispensible and insist you be transferred internally where you will double your salary. If they get promoted you get promoted, if they go off and start a new company you are the first hire. This can be tricky because of signing multiple NDAs even between internal departments but if you are serious about wanting to learn they will tell management to make it happen. I actually took all the above advice from a comedian many years ago and my life would not be the same if I hadn't watched that throwaway clip at 3am because I always assumed there was no way I could ever do this kind of work.

If you're not interested in finance there are a few companies that mentor developers sometimes even direct from local high schools and build them to senior developers. One is in New Hampshire called Northwoods Software. If you live in Egypt, Georgia, North Macedonia, Albania, Latvia, or Bosnia you can work remotely for Scandiweb (Latvia). The pay is only 500-600 Euros per month but you don't know anything yet so are being paid for direct mentorship to become a senior developer.

If you contribute to open source projects they will teach you as well and you will pick up work for example while writing this workshop I tried out contributing to Blender then made a LinkedIn profile claiming to be a self-taught student and C++ developer with a single small feature as my portfolio. Numerous local gaming companies tried to recruit me shortly after saying they would train and offering all kinds of signing bonuses. LinkedIn is trash I've only ever used it to harvest recruiter offers. Of course not all fintech outfits are worth working at, do your own research.

Explains that almost all industry popular languages are virtually identical and demystifies all the OOP jargon. We use a web-based stacker to see exactly how programs execute.

  • Conceptual design book The Essence of Software

MIT course and book on how to design software people want to use.

  • Queueing theory

If you write enough software eventually you discover that almost everything is some kind of queue pattern. This is another workbook that's Q&A style not a giant theory text.

  • Advanced DBMS (OLAP)

Here we learn kernel bypass methods for low latency networking, code profiling and optimization, hashing/join algorithms, search space complexity reduction, and modern distributed application architecture like microservices. We also learn cache memory hierarchy and systems. We can also look at their AI tuning seminar for relational dbms optimization knob tuning. Most of the assignments are finishing features on github for a teaching database with a rust written optimizer but there's nothing stopping you from using whatever language you prefer.

Real competitive programming

Here is Gennady Korotkevich aka tourist practicing on codeforces and explaining what he's doing, though he didn't start with C++ he used to win the IOI with Pascal in high school then switched later. The first thing to notice is he's using a basic text editor and minimalist shell that looks like MinGW a Windows port of GCC and Far Manager where he manually submits through the codeforces website UI. std::cin is synchronized to std::cout and performs default buffer flushing operations so to save some runtime he disables this sync. His compiler flags he explains in one of these recorded streams, most of them are to make compiler messages extra verbose for debugging. Note you would never write software outside of competitive programming like this because he has opened a gigantic scope.

You don't have to watch the entire 2hr vid, the last problem seems like the most informative. He begins most problems by writing examples as comments. He copies the problem input test cases into a text file 'in1' then pipes it into his compiled program "sol.exe < in1" from the shell as stdin (standard input). A TLE or time limit exceeded verdict shows how he would optimize to get accepted verdict also trolling the stream by making all the variable names into single letters claiming it will speed up the program. The judging server ran his program faster than his laptop, something to remember.

Big-O explained

The USACO guide has a good introduction to big-O and a resource we can use where running time is actually explained is the beginning chapters of Functional Data Structures by Prof Ragde at Waterloo try reading the first few chapters up to 2.2.

The formal definition is:

\(f(x) \le c\cdot g(x)\) for all inputs \(x \ge x_0\) as x (inputs) goes to infinity. If that inequality holds then f(x) is in O(g(x)) which represents a worse-case scenario upper bound. A 'best-case' (wishful thinking) scenario for insertion sort is you don't have to do anything but attach the element to the already sorted list and the worst-case is the entire list is in reverse order and every element looked at and moved.

f() function is a cost function of time or resources derived from a cost model. It's where you count up every constant operation and loop costs by analyzing source. This is not always easy since what costs do you assign when the code triggers garbage collection or triggers memory allocation because an object has been created deep in some library code.

The g() function is chosen from a list of math functions that represent a 'family of functions'. A family means all functions that are linear, all functions that are quadratic, all functions that are linearithmic etc. It actually is a specific list as we'll soon see.

Why are constants hidden in big-O? If you read SICP their example is 16 and 8 both differ by a factor of 2 and 8 but we don't care which factor. In real life the constant factors do matter if they are hiding a lot of operations. A classic example of this is when Knuth was writing the Stanford GraphBase (contents are in Vol 4A of TAOCP) and his real-world testing of popular graph algorithms found that 'Fibonacci heaps' that were analyzed using a technique called amortized analysis weren't any better than Kruskal's old algorithm because the analysis was hiding large constant operations. One way to reduce constants is to do consecutive rounds of a linear algorithm such as cleaning up a graph removing cycles because O(n + n) or O(2n) is still O(n).

Big-O doesn't represent growth since if f(x) = x2 it does not grow to be quadratic, f(x) already is quadratic, thus f() is in \(O(n^{2})\) or upper bounded by every quadratic function. Typically in algorithm analysis the inputs are supposed to be 'near infinite' which is why n is used instead of x in f(n) to signal the difference in inputs but here in the USACO guide we can see that if the input is small then we can get away with some very bad runtimes even exponential.

Problem complexity

Before we start trying to solve problems watch this Erik Demaine lecture and skip to 09:00. We want to know what polynomial time is, what NP means, what satisfiability means and this is a good enough ~30m crash course in complexity as these concepts come up all the time in algorithm literature. He tells us most decision problems can't be solved by an algorithm which ruins the AI superintelligence dystopia. When he translates Super Mario Bros to a 3SAT decision problem we can see that almost every algorithm you can think of can be transformed to a decision problem. You don't have to keep watching this but there's an interesting intro to constraint logic/graphs. Now you can explain to someone P=NP means all the nondeterministic polynomial time algorithms that always 'lucky guess' correctly or have a magic oracle can be solved by an algorithm in deterministic polynomial time which seems impossible.

The rest of this course I won't do here but the book he wrote that goes with the lectures looks excellent covering topics in economics too.

Software Design

MIT has a course 6.1040 Software Design and a book The Essence of Software. It used to be called 6.170 before MIT renumbered every course recently. Universities always do ridiculous things every 3-5 years it's like some new dean comes in and demands oh we have to reinvent everything and buy all new (awful) edtech.

First look at these slides from 2023 titled Diverge-Converge it briefly explains how you go from brainstorming features of something you want to build into concepts.

Click here, skip the 'message of the day', and scroll to the very bottom of the materials list where there are 6 lecture videos. They are all worth watching but we are interested in the two lectures on conceptual design. Watch lecture 1 and lecture 2. Look at the recent slides. You have surely encountered badly designed software and this is a way to model your ideas as state machines then synch them together to compose an application that people can actually understand how to use. If you don't understand what you just looked at don't worry we'll be implementing it in full you will get it.

Gitless

Now we are going to take all the above introductions to conceptual design and rewrite the git interface to make it intuitive to use. We have to learn git anyway so may as well write a CLI (command line interface) or you could make it ridiculous and call it 'visual git' or something with DAG graphics.

Many companies today use a customized Mercurial fork or rewritten clone instead of git like Meta's rust written EdenFS and Sapling SCM because git was originally developed to take advantage of linux file system optimizations ergo is conceptually attached to a file system while Mercurial can be easily adapted to be stuffed in a database, distributed or run on any OS. In a Mercurial style of version control, you probably don't care about individual commits so you don't even add comments to commits you instead care about entire branch development.

Let's learn what git actually is by watching MIT's version control lecture from it's course The Missing Semester of Your CS Education. Lecture starts ~7m in. So far the data structure and model is trivial to understand if you did any intro course here such as CS19 learnings about DAGs.

Proceed to gitless.com and navigate to research or this page on MIT's software design group. You will find this talk about the problems with git design. The guy giving this talk was the PhD student of the prof we watched in the conceptual design lectures and now is a co-founder of Aleph some fintech data startup (which is hiring, maybe you can impress him with your conceptual design skills).
